Each concept is one stop in the pipeline that turns raw numbers into intelligent-looking behavior. II · Numbers

Everything starts as a number.

Before there are weights, layers, or embeddings — there are floating-point numbers. The format you choose decides how much memory the model takes, how fast it runs, and how precise it can be.

A floating-point number is three pieces of information packed together: sign · exponent · mantissa.

A model with 70 billion parameters in FP32 needs 280 GB of memory just to hold its weights. In FP8, that drops to 70 GB. This is why every modern model is shipped in a smaller format than it was trained in.

III · Parameters

A neuron is a weighted sum.

Every parameter in a neural network is either a weight — how much an input matters — or a bias — a baseline value added in. That's it. Billions of them, but always one of those two things.

That's one neuron. A modern frontier model has on the order of 10¹¹ of these, organized into layers. Training is the process of finding the values of all those weights and biases — by gradient descent — so that the network's outputs match what we want.

IV · The Operation

Almost everything is a matrix multiply.

One operation does the bulk of the work inside any neural network. Take a vector of inputs, multiply by a matrix of learned weights, add a bias vector, apply a non-linearity. Repeat in layers. Compose those layers into a block. Stack the blocks.

That's the entire operation. Real layers have weight matrices that are thousands by thousands; a single B200 GPU does ~4.5 × 10¹⁵ of these multiplies per second at FP8. Almost every joule of training and inference energy is spent right here.

Each block transforms a sequence of vectors into another sequence of the same shape, just richer. Modern frontier models stack 60–120 of these. The output of the final block, run through one more linear projection, becomes the logits.

Every weight in the entire 70B-parameter model lives somewhere in one of these boxes — distributed across N layers. The attention block is what makes a transformer a transformer; everything else has been around since the 80s. We'll look at attention next.

V · Attention

Each token looks at every other.

Attention is the single mechanism that makes transformers work. For each token in the sequence, ask: which other tokens matter for understanding this one? Add up the relevant ones, weighted by how relevant. That's it. The whole field hinges on this one idea from 2017.

Self-attention, made visible

Click any token in "the cat sat on the warm mat" to make it the query. The heatmap shows where the query token's attention flows — which other tokens it pulls information from.

13ATTENTION
attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q · Kᵀ / √d) · V
Attention matrix · row = query, column = key
From "cat", attention flows to:

Each token has three learned vectors: a query (what am I looking for?), a key (what do I offer?), and a value (the information I'd pass along). Multiply every query by every key, soften with softmax, weighted-sum the values. That's a single attention head. Real models run dozens of these in parallel and stack the result through many layers — and somehow that turns into language.

VII · Embeddings

Words become coordinates.

An embedding turns something messy — a word, an image, a sentence — into a list of numbers. A vector. And vectors live in space, with directions and distances. Suddenly we can do geometry on meaning.

The semantic plane

Real embeddings have hundreds or thousands of dimensions. Here are 30 words flattened to two. Notice that semantic neighbors cluster together.

03SPACE

The fact that "king" is close to "queen" — and that the direction from "man" to "woman" is similar to the direction from "king" to "queen" — is the foundation of every modern AI system that "understands" text. It's also exactly how vector search works.

IX · Quantization

Smaller numbers, most of the smarts.

Models are trained in high precision but they don't need that precision to run. Quantization replaces each weight with a smaller, coarser approximation. The model gets smaller and faster; the quality, mostly, holds.

X · The Machine

All of this runs on silicon.

Every weight, every multiplication, every gradient update happens on a physical chip. The numbers get vivid: 208 billion switches on one piece of glass, datacenters drinking gigawatts, clusters that need their own power plants.

XI · The Grid

The bottleneck isn't chips. It's power.

You can buy the GPUs. Getting a gigawatt of electricity to them — reliably, on a schedule — is now the hard part. The AI race has quietly become an energy-and-infrastructure race, decided by grid queues, transformers, geography, and the metals underneath all of it.

Load is growing faster than transmission can be permitted and built, so power availability has become the primary screen for a serious project — and the reason developers increasingly bring their own generation instead of waiting in line.

This is the cascade the headlines skip. A better model needs more compute; more compute needs more power; more power needs transformers, turbines, and transmission; and those need copper, electrical steel, gallium, and rare earths — dug out of the ground on mining timelines, not chip timelines. The furthest-upstream constraint on artificial intelligence is, oddly, geology.

XII · The Stack

The constraints that actually shape AI.

Compute is what gets press, but compute isn't the bottleneck most of the time. Memory bandwidth is. Interconnect bandwidth is. Where the data lives, and how fast you can move it, decides what's possible.

The memory hierarchy

Every step down the ladder trades speed for size. The art of fast inference is keeping the data you need close to where you're computing. Hover any name for what it is.

So the bar and the number are two views of the same fact: a long bar is a short wait. Register to SSD spans about six orders of magnitude — a register answers in 0.3 ns, an SSD in ~50,000 ns. This latency gap is why LLM serving is called "memory-bound": the GPU often finishes its math waiting on the next weights to arrive from HBM.

XIII · Training

How the weights got there.

A model starts with random numbers. Training is the process of nudging every one of those billions of numbers in the direction that makes the loss go down. Repeat for months. Across the cluster you just costed.

The engine that does the nudging is gradient descent. Picture the model's error as a landscape: every possible setting of the weights is a point on the ground, and the height at that point is how wrong the model is. You want the lowest valley. You can't see the whole map — there are billions of dimensions — but at wherever you're standing you can feel which way is downhill. That downhill direction is the gradient. Take a step that way, feel again, step again. That loop, repeated trillions of times, is how every modern model learns.

It matters because it is the only known way to fit a model with hundreds of billions of parameters. Nobody hand-sets those numbers; there are far too many. Gradient descent is what turns raw compute and data into a working model — the reason more GPUs and more tokens buy you a better model at all. The method itself is old: the mathematician Augustin-Louis Cauchy wrote it down in 1847. What made it work for neural networks was backpropagation, popularized in 1986 — a way to compute the gradient for every weight in one efficient sweep. The core idea hasn't changed since.

Real training is this same loop, just in a billion dimensions instead of two. The loss is averaged over thousands of examples per step ("mini-batch"). But the core idea — step downhill by gradient × learning rate — is the same one from 1847, made practical for neural networks in 1986.

Scaling laws — loss as a function of compute

The remarkable finding: how good a model gets is largely predictable from how much compute you spend. Hover any curve.

20SCALING

First, the phrase. Loss is the model's average error — how surprised it is by the next token, one number that falls as it gets better. Compute is the total arithmetic spent training it, measured in FLOPs (roughly GPUs × time). "Loss as a function of compute" just means plotting one against the other: spend the x-axis, get the y-axis. The startling result, from OpenAI's 2020 scaling-laws paper, is that this plot is a clean straight line on log axes — a power law. Error falls in a smooth, predictable way as you add compute, parameters, and data. That predictability is what let labs justify nine-figure training runs before starting: you could forecast the payoff.

The amber line is the compute-optimal frontier — DeepMind's 2022 "Chinchilla" refinement: for a fixed budget, you minimize loss with roughly 20 tokens per parameter. Too few parameters and you overtrain a small model; too many and you starve a big one of data. The 2020–2024 frontier era was largely an exercise in surfing this curve.

XIV · Output

From numbers back to language.

The model's final layer doesn't output a word. It outputs a logit — a raw score — for every possible next token. Softmax turns those scores into probabilities. Then a sampling rule picks one.

Four words do all the work here, so plainly:

Logit
The raw score the model assigns each candidate next token before any normalizing — often just called the "score." Can be negative. There is one logit per token in the vocabulary (~100,000 of them). Bigger logit = the model likes that token more.
Temperature
A dial that divides the logits before softmax. Low (→0) sharpens the distribution toward the single top token — safe, repetitive, deterministic. High (>1) flattens it so unlikely tokens get a real chance — creative, and more error-prone. The model doesn't change; only how boldly you read it.
Top-K
Before sampling, throw away everything except the K highest-scoring tokens, then pick from those. A fixed-size shortlist. K=1 means always take the top token (greedy).
Top-Pnucleus
Keep the smallest set of top tokens whose probabilities add up to P (say 0.9), then pick from those. The shortlist resizes itself: short when the model is confident, long when it's unsure. The production default for most chat models.

XVI · Quick Reference

The vocabulary.

Everything we've touched, in a sentence each.

Tensor
A multi-dimensional array of numbers. The data structure that everything in a neural network is. A scalar is 0D, a vector 1D, a matrix 2D, a tensor anything beyond.
Weight
A learned number that scales how much one input contributes to one output. Most parameters in a model are weights.
Bias
A learned constant added after the weighted sum. Lets a neuron output something other than zero when all inputs are zero.
Parameter
A weight or a bias. The numbers the model learns during training and uses during inference. "70B parameters" means 70 billion of these.
Activation
The output of a layer for a specific input — the intermediate values flowing through the model right now. Unlike parameters, these change with every input.
Gradient
The direction of steepest increase of the loss with respect to a parameter. Training nudges each parameter in the opposite direction.
Floating point
A way to store real numbers in a fixed number of bits using sign, exponent, and mantissa. Modern AI runs on FP32, FP16, BF16, FP8, and below.
Quantization
Replacing high-precision weights with lower-precision approximations to save memory and compute. INT4 is common in production.
Embedding
A vector that represents an input in a learned space where geometric distance reflects semantic similarity.
Vector
An ordered list of numbers. In ML, almost always a point in a high-dimensional space representing something.
Dimension
One coordinate axis of a vector space. A 1536-dim embedding has 1536 numbers in it. More dimensions = more room for nuance.
Cosine similarity
A way to compare two vectors by the angle between them. Ranges from -1 to 1. The standard metric for vector search.
Vector search
Finding the K closest vectors to a query vector in a large database. Powered by indexes like HNSW that avoid checking every point.
Latent space
The internal vector space where a model represents its inputs. Often surprisingly meaningful — directions correspond to concepts.
Token
The discrete unit a language model processes. Usually a sub-word, a few characters. "Unbelievable" might be three tokens.
Logit
The raw, unnormalized score the model produces for each possible next token. Negative logits are possible.
Softmax
A function that turns a list of numbers into a probability distribution. Exponentiate, then normalize so they sum to 1.
Temperature
A divisor applied to logits before softmax. Higher temperature = flatter distribution = more random output.
Matrix multiplication
The single dominant operation in a neural network. A layer's output = its weight matrix times its input vector. Roughly 99% of inference compute.
Forward pass
Running an input through every layer of the model to produce an output. What happens every time you ask a model anything.
Transistor
A microscopic switch — the atomic unit of digital computation. A modern AI GPU has ~208 billion of them, each a few dozen atoms across.
Die
A single piece of silicon cut from a wafer. Modern GPUs use multi-die packaging — two or more dies bonded together — because one die has hit the reticle size limit (~800 mm²).
Process node
A generation of chip manufacturing technology, named in nanometers ("3nm", "4nm"). The names stopped corresponding to physical size around 2015. What's real is transistor density: ~130–300 M/mm².
FLOPS
Floating-point operations per second. The unit of compute throughput. A single B200 does ~5 PFLOPS (10¹⁵) at FP8. Modern AI clusters hit tens of EFLOPS (10¹⁸).
HBM
High-Bandwidth Memory. DRAM stacked vertically and placed right next to a GPU die, with a wide interface. The B200 carries 192 GB of HBM3e at 8 TB/s.
Tensor core
A specialized matrix-multiply unit inside modern GPUs. NVIDIA's 5th-gen tensor cores handle FP4, FP6, FP8, and FP16 with hardware sparsity support.
TDP
Thermal Design Power — the steady-state heat a chip is expected to dissipate. The B200 is rated at 1,000 W, up from the H100's 700 W.
Data center
A facility housing thousands to millions of servers. AI data centers are increasingly purpose-built: liquid-cooled, gigawatt-scale, often with their own power generation.
PUE
Power Usage Effectiveness — how much total power a data center draws per watt of useful compute. Good modern facilities: 1.1–1.3. The overhead is cooling, lighting, networking, losses.
Self-attention
The mechanism at the heart of every transformer. Each token computes a weighted sum over every other token, where the weights come from a learned compatibility function.
Q, K, V
Query, key, value. For each token, the model produces three learned vectors. Queries × keys give attention weights; those weights are applied to values.
Multi-head attention
Running several independent attention operations in parallel, each with its own learned Q/K/V projections. Each head specializes in different relationships.
Residual connection
Adding a layer's input back to its output. Lets gradients flow through deep networks without vanishing. Every transformer block has two.
LayerNorm
Normalizing activations to have mean 0 and variance 1 across a vector. Stabilizes training of deep networks.
FFN / MLP
The feed-forward network inside each transformer block. Two linear layers with a non-linearity between. Roughly two-thirds of a model's parameters live here.
BPE
Byte-Pair Encoding — the algorithm behind most modern tokenizers. Starts from raw bytes, iteratively merges the most common adjacent pairs into single tokens.
KV cache
During generation, keys and values from previous tokens are cached so they don't need to be recomputed. Grows linearly with context length; dominates serving memory for long contexts.
Mixture of experts
An architecture where each token activates only a sparse subset of the model's parameters via a learned router. Lets total capacity be enormous while per-token compute stays modest.
Scaling laws
Empirical power-law relationships between loss, parameters, data, and compute. Chinchilla showed the optimal ratio is ~20 training tokens per parameter.
Learning rate
How big a step gradient descent takes in the direction of steepest descent. Too small and training is slow; too large and it diverges.
Momentum
An optimizer trick that adds a fraction of the previous step to the current step. Lets training move faster through long shallow valleys.
All-reduce
The collective communication that sums gradients across every GPU in a training cluster, after every batch. The bandwidth pattern that motivates NVLink and InfiniBand.
NVLink
NVIDIA's proprietary high-bandwidth interconnect between GPUs. Generation 5 hits 1.8 TB/s per GPU — about 14× PCIe Gen5.
Top-K / Top-P
Sampling strategies. Top-K keeps the K most likely tokens; Top-P keeps the smallest set summing to probability P. Both renormalize and sample.
Gradient descent
The optimization loop behind all training: measure the slope of the loss, step downhill, repeat. Written down by Cauchy in 1847; still the core of how every model learns.
Backpropagation
The chain rule applied through every layer to compute the gradient for all weights in one backward sweep. Popularized for neural nets in 1986; what makes gradient descent tractable at scale.
Availability zone
One or more isolated data centers within a cloud region, with independent power and cooling. Apps span several so one failure doesn't take everything down. AI training does the opposite — it packs into one site.
Interconnection queue
The regulated line to connect a new large load or power plant to the grid. Now years long in the US, it has become the binding constraint on data-center growth — more than chips or capital.
Neocloud
A cloud provider built only around GPUs — bare-metal, InfiniBand, cheaper than hyperscalers. CoreWeave, Lambda, Crusoe, Nebius. Even hyperscalers rent from them to fill capacity gaps.
